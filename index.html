Featured case study

Rebuilding a Founder's Week

A time audit and schedule redesign for a UK SME founder wearing seven hats.

Client UK SME founder · ~15 employees
Industry D2C e-commerce + B2B hardware
Tools used Excel, Asana, Outlook, Word
Status Live · follow-up audit scheduled

Client context

The client is the founder and director of a UK-based small-to-medium enterprise operating in both direct-to-consumer e-commerce and B2B hardware. The company has around 15 employees across operations, customer service, web development, sales, and warehouse functions.

The structural issue: the founder personally holds seven seats on the accountability chart. He is the Visionary and the Integrator (the dual founder role most CEOs eventually split) and he also directly owns the Finance, IT, and E-commerce departments. Only Operations and B2B Sales have been fully handed off to other leaders.

Seven seats means seven distinct cognitive modes — finance brain, IT brain, strategy brain, e-commerce brain — competing for the same brain on the same day. Context-switching is structural, not a failure of discipline.

The problem

Three signals prompted the audit:

  • Workday drift. Despite a stated 5:00 PM hard stop, the founder routinely worked until 5:30 PM. The extension added 2.5 hours per week of low-output time and compounded into fatigue the following day.
  • Fragmented focus. The EA observed the founder jumping between tasks within single deep-work blocks — pulling up Veeqo mid-finance review, responding to Slack pings mid-strategy session. The pattern was visible but not yet measured.
  • Strategic work crowded out. Visionary work — the founder's highest-leverage activity — kept getting pushed to evenings or weekends because the operational seats consumed weekday hours.

The business cost showed up in three places: slower decision-making (because reactive work crowded strategic thinking), late evening work bleeding into family time, and the founder describing himself as "always behind on something." Growth had not stalled, but the founder was increasingly the bottleneck on multiple fronts.

The approach

Audit method

A 5-day time-tracking exercise over a normal working week. The founder logged activity into 5 buckets every 30-60 minutes:

  • Work (any business-related activity)
  • Sleep
  • Personal activities (meals, family, hobbies, personal admin)
  • Miscellaneous (transit, errands, dog walks, household)
  • Unrecorded (gaps in the log)

The audit captured all 120 hours of the working week, not just business hours. This was deliberate. A schedule that protects work time without acknowledging sleep, lunch, dog walks, and recovery will fail within weeks.

Analysis method

Bucket-level data only. The audit didn't sub-divide work into meetings vs. emails vs. deep work, because the founder's self-tracking wasn't granular enough to support that. Inventing a breakdown would have produced false precision. The analysis worked from what the data actually showed.

Productivity windows were identified by cross-referencing the time log against the founder's subjective energy ratings, captured in the same tracking sheet.

Key findings

  • Working hours overrun. Daily work averaged 7h 23m against a stated 9:30 AM–5:00 PM target (6h 30m after lunch). The 30-minute drift each evening was real and consistent.
  • Two productivity peaks, two dips. Output concentrated at 9–10 AM and 2–4 PM. Dips at 12–1 PM and 4–5 PM. The 4–5 PM dip was where most of the daily extension into 5:30 PM was landing — adding time with diminishing returns.
  • Personal and work bled together during peak hours. Errands and dog walks were happening at 2–3 PM, the second-strongest productivity window.
  • Comms checked throughout the day. No designated comms windows. Email and Slack got checked reactively, fragmenting focus across every work block.
  • Sleep at 7h 11m. Healthy but at the lower edge given the cognitive demands of running seven seats.

The founder's instinct about his own productivity peaks was correct. What was wrong wasn't the timing, it was the protection. Peak windows were being interrupted by comms, errands, and unscheduled requests. The solution wasn't to teach him when to focus; it was to defend the windows when his energy was already aligned.

The recommendations

Structural changes

  • Day-themed weeks. Monday Finance, Tuesday Vision, Wednesday Leadership, Thursday E-commerce, Friday Review. Reduces context-switching by giving the brain a primary mode each day.
  • Tuesday as protected visionary day. Out of office, zero meetings (including with the EA). One full day per week of uninterrupted strategic work — 6.5 hours.
  • Hard stop at 5:00 PM. EA-enforced through calendar holds and a daily 4:55 PM reminder. The 4:30–5:00 PM block reassigned to low-cognitive-load tasks so the day finishes cleanly.
  • Lunch + dog walk locked at 1:00–2:00 PM daily. A personal non-negotiable that doubles as a midday reset.

Behavioural changes

  • Two designated comms windows per day. 10:00–10:30 AM and 4:00–4:30 PM. Slack DND and email notifications off outside these windows.
  • Email-free Tuesday morning. First comms check pushed to 4:00 PM, giving six consecutive hours of strategic work before the inbox primes the wrong cognitive mode.
  • Post-leadership-meeting buffer block. 15 minutes after the weekly L10 to let discussion settle before diving into execution.
  • EA daily catch-up reframed as a focus audit. The 9:30 AM standup now includes "What did you produce in each deep work block yesterday?" Inability to answer flags drift, addressed without shame.

Environmental changes

  • Pre-staged deep work blocks. EA sets up the environment before each block — relevant tabs open, others closed, phone away. Five minutes of prep, large payoff.
  • Visible task anchor. Current task sticky-noted on the monitor for each block. External reminder to defeat internal drift.
  • Inbox firewall. EA triages email twice daily. Founder sees only what genuinely needs his attention.

Systems built

  • Rebuilt Asana project with 57 time-blocked tasks covering the full week, plus a weekly template task for one-click duplication each Friday
  • Meeting agenda subtasks for the EA catch-up, weekly bookkeeping 1:1, and L10 — so meetings have structure rather than starting from scratch each week
  • 32 recurring Outlook events (delivered as a single .ics import) covering all meetings, comms windows, lunch blocks, and hard-stop reminders through end of 2027
  • Tracking tasks for the first 4 weeks — daily 5 PM exit log and Tuesday adherence check — with structured templates for capturing data
  • Follow-up time audit scheduled 6 weeks post-implementation to measure outcomes against the original baseline

Outcome measurement

The new schedule went live in May 2026. Outcome measurement is in progress — the follow-up time audit runs in late June, with the first quarterly review in mid-August.

Qualitative measures being tracked: founder's subjective focus rating during deep work blocks, Tuesday adherence (clean visionary day yes/no), and whether the 4-5 PM dip flattens when filled with low-cognitive-load tasks instead of attempted deep work.

Reflections

What worked in the process

  • Designing around the founder's existing peaks rather than imposing an idealised schedule. The original schedule failed because it ignored when his energy was actually available.
  • Combining the audit with environmental and behavioural fixes. A schedule on its own would not solve distractibility.
  • Treating the EA as the enforcement layer, not the founder. Self-enforced 5 PM stops fail. EA-enforced ones don't.

What I'd do differently next time

  • Track work-time sub-categories from the start. The 5-bucket method captured the macro picture but not what the work hours actually contained.
  • Build the tracking system at the same time as the schedule, not after. The measurement layer should ship with version one.
A note on methodology: This case study is published while outcome measurement is in progress. All baseline numbers are from the actual 5-day audit. All target numbers are explicit goals of the intervention, not claimed results. This document will be updated after the follow-up audit with measured outcomes.

Recruitment case study

11 roles, 4,000+ applicants, one pipeline

End-to-end recruitment for a UK SME — designed to keep supervisors out of the recruitment weeds.

Client UK SME · ~15 employees
Industry D2C e-commerce + B2B hardware
Timeframe Approximately 12 months
Tools used Indeed, Asana, Outlook, MS Teams, MS Office

The problem

Before I joined, recruitment at the company ran on supervisor time. Each open role pulled department heads away from their actual jobs to draft listings, sift CVs, and schedule interviews. The process was slow, manual, and inconsistent. Hiring decisions were rushed because supervisors had to fit them around their operational duties.

The brief was simple: design a pipeline that gives supervisors a shortlist they trust, and takes everything before and after the interview off their plate.

The approach

I built a standardised recruitment pipeline that handled every stage end-to-end except the interviews themselves. My scope covered sourcing, evaluation, shortlisting, coordination, and onboarding. Direct supervisors only stepped in at the interview stage to pick from a pre-vetted shortlist.

The pipeline

  • Drafting job descriptions, application assessments, and application forms
  • Setting evaluation criteria specific to each role
  • Posting roles — Indeed for onsite roles, social media communities and VA groups for remote roles
  • Applicant screening and evaluation against criteria
  • Shortlisting with full evaluation reports, logged in Asana with attached CVs and application materials
  • Coordinating interview schedules between applicants and supervisors
  • Sending rejection letters to applicants who didn't make the cut
  • Coordinating assessment sessions, trial sessions, and final interviews
  • Drafting contractor agreements and onboarding documents
  • Setting up accounts and tools access for new hires
  • Onboarding sessions — company intro, team intros, tool walkthroughs

The numbers

Across 11 roles over roughly 12 months, the pipeline processed more than 4,000 applications. The funnel for each role:

  • 156 shortlisted candidates with full evaluation reports
  • 68 interviews coordinated end-to-end
  • 12 hires completed; 3 roles still active

Cost discipline

Indeed spend was kept deliberately tight. For high-volume roles like warehouse operatives, sponsored listings were necessary to surface the role above local competition. For specialist remote roles, I sourced through social media VA communities at zero ad spend.

Retention notes

Of the 12 completed hires, 7 are still in role at the time of writing. The 5 early departures were a mix of role-fit issues and external factors: candidates who interviewed well but couldn't keep up with technical demands on the job, performance issues uncovered post-hire, one transfer to a full-time role elsewhere, and one cost-cutting decision unrelated to the candidate's performance.

These weren't ignored. Each departure informed updates to the screening criteria — for example, after a customer service hire struggled with the computer skills required for the role, a basic computer literacy check was added to the screening process for that role going forward.

What changed for the client

  • Supervisor time recovered. Direct supervisors no longer spent hours each week on sourcing, screening, or interview coordination. They opened Asana, reviewed the shortlist, picked who to interview, and showed up. That was the extent of their involvement.
  • Standardised evaluation. Every shortlisted candidate came with a written evaluation report against role-specific criteria. Decisions were made on consistent grounds rather than gut feel mid-interview.
  • Onboarding handled in full. New hires arrived with contracts signed, accounts created, tools provisioned, and an orientation already scheduled. The supervisor's first interaction was operational, not administrative.
  • Documented and repeatable. The pipeline is now a documented system, not a one-person operation. The next person who runs it doesn't start from scratch.

What I'd do differently next time

  • Build the screening criteria with more emphasis on practical task tests, especially for roles where on-the-job skills don't always show in CVs or interviews.
  • Track retention as a first-class metric from day one, with structured 30/60/90-day check-ins built into the pipeline rather than added later.
A note on scope: My role covered sourcing, evaluation, shortlisting, coordination, contracts, and onboarding. Interviews were conducted by the direct supervisors for each role. Hiring decisions were theirs to make from the shortlist I prepared.

B2B documentation case study

A spec sheet system the B2B team actually uses

58 product data sheets, two brand systems, one repeatable template — turning ad-hoc requests into a 30-minute turnaround.

Scope 58 product data sheets and O&M manuals, ongoing
Industry B2B hardware · multi-brand
Turnaround 15–30 minutes per sheet
Tools used Canva, SharePoint, company website

The problem

B2B customers routinely requested formal product data sheets alongside quotations. The team had an older format built in Microsoft Word — functional, but poorly arranged, inconsistent across products, and slow to produce. Each request pulled someone from the B2B team into layout work rather than client work.

The result was a bottleneck. Spec sheets were either rushed, delayed, or sent over in a state that didn't match the quality of the products they were selling.

A spec sheet is often the last thing a B2B buyer sees before signing off. It either reinforces the quote or undermines it. The old format was undermining.

What I built

I rebuilt the format from scratch in Canva, designing a master template that could handle the company's full product range while staying readable and on-brand. The redesign improved both the visual structure and the editorial standards — clearer headings, consistent terminology, properly weighted technical detail.

Template structure

Every sheet follows the same two-page A4 layout, regardless of product:

What each sheet covers

  • Header band with brand logo, accreditations, and trust marks
  • Product title block with hero imagery and supporting shots
  • Materials, coating, security features, and finish options
  • Standards and compliance — EN 13724:2002, BS 8300:2009, EN 13501-1 fire ratings, ISO 9001
  • Numbering, lock options, custom orders, and after-sales terms
  • Technical data table on page 2 — dimensions, slot sizes, installation options, RAL colour codes
  • Branded footer with full contact details across phone, email, web, and address

Multi-brand handling

The same product family is sold under two distinct brand systems with different visual identities — a primary blue-and-white brand and a secondary brand with co-branded partner marks. The template was built to flex between brand systems without losing structural consistency.

The same approach extended to longer documents: Operation & Maintenance manuals, installation safety guides, and technical specification booklets — all built on the same layout grid so the documentation feels coherent across the entire product range.

Handling technical and regulatory content

Spec sheets aren't decorative. They contain claims B2B buyers rely on for compliance. The work required careful handling of:

  • European standards. EN 13724:2002 for letterbox aperture compliance, BS 8300:2009 +A1 for wheelchair accessibility, EN 13501-1 fire classification (Class A2-s3, d2 expected based on material properties).
  • Material specifications. AISI 316L stainless steel grades, 0.7mm galvanised carbon steel with zinc coating microns, Armaflex insulation thickness, powder coat density and autoignition temperatures.
  • Distinguishing tested vs expected compliance. Where the company holds certified test results, the sheets state them directly. Where compliance is expected based on material properties but not individually tested, the sheets say so — explicitly noted as an assumption, not a certification. That precision protects the client from misrepresentation.

The workflow

A B2B team member raises a request when they need a sheet for a quotation. I draft from the master template, pulling product information directly from the company website, and deliver within 30 minutes. The B2B team proofreads, edits if needed, and sends it to the client.

All finished sheets are filed in a structured SharePoint folder organised by brand and product family. The B2B team can pull existing sheets directly without going through me when the product hasn't changed.

What changed for the client

  • Turnaround time collapsed. From "we'll send it tomorrow" to delivered within the same half-hour the request was made.
  • B2B team stays in client work. Layout and formatting are no longer their problem. They request, proofread, send.
  • Consistency across the catalog. 58 sheets and counting that look and read like they belong to the same company.
  • Self-serve archive. The SharePoint library means most sheets are already on hand. New requests only happen for products that didn't have one yet, or where details changed.

What I'd improve next

  • Build a metadata layer on top of the SharePoint folder so spec sheets can be searched by product family, certification, or material rather than browsed.
  • Create a request intake form to standardise the brief from the B2B team — currently briefs vary, which slows down the harder sheets.
